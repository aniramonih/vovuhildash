Ivan Lendl: Style of Play

Lendl was a pioneer of the aggressive baseline style of play that has become so popular in recent years.

Ivan Lendl: Career

Early Career

As a junior, Lendl drew the attention of the tennis world.

He was the world No. 1 junior player in 1978, winning the boys’ singles titles at both the French Open and Wimbledon.

The tennis player won 15 of the 23 singles tournaments in 1982, putting up a 44-match winning streak.

In 1978, Lendl began his professional career and reached his first top-level singles final in 1979. Similarly, he won seven solo titles in 1980. 

Ivan was a major force behind his country’s squad. His abilities and skills led the Czechoslovak team to win the World Cup in 1981.

In, Later years, however, they finished second in 1984 and 1985. 

However, after moving to the US in 1986, he stopped competing in these events since the Tennis Association of Czechoslovakia regarded him as an “illegal defector” from their nation.

Even in his early years, the tennis star has won numerous trophies.

With his title wins, Lendl immediately became the highest-paid tennis player of all time.

In 1983, he won seven more events; nonetheless, he had yet to win a Grand Slam championship during his early career.

Ivan reached his first Grand Slam final, losing to Björn Borg in five sets at the French Open.

Prime Years

In 1984, Ivan won his first Grand Slam title at the French Open, defeating John McEnroe in a marathon final.

The tennis player won the French Open in 1986 and 1987 and the season-ending Masters Grand Prix titles in 1986 and 1987, defeating Becker in straight sets in 1986 and Wilander in three sets in 1987. 

From 1985 through 1987, Lendl’s match-winning rate was over 90%.

Lendl reached ten consecutive Grand Slam singles semifinals from the 1985 US Open to the 1988 Australian Open, which Federer broke at the 2007 Australian Open.

Wimbledon was the only Grand Slam singles title Lendl never won. 

He reached the final twice after reaching the semi-finals in 1983 and 1984.

Later Years

He skipped the French Open once more to concentrate on Wimbledon, but he lost in the third round to David Wheaton and never won the tournament.

Due to chronic back discomfort, Lendl announced his retirement from professional tennis on December 21, 1994, at 34. 

His last professional match was three and a half months before his retirement, in which he lost in the US Open’s second round in 1994.

The tennis player, nicknamed “The Terminator,” won a massive 94 ATP singles titles and 6 doubles titles and earned approximately 22 million dollars for his efforts.

In addition, the champion got inducted into the Tennis Hall of Fame in 2001.

Coaching Years

On December 31, 2011, Lendl was named Andy Murray’s coach.

Murray’s maturity and consistency have been credited to Lendl, who guided him to his first two Grand Slam triumphs at the 2012 US Open and 2013 Wimbledon Championships.

Lendl and Murray, the partnership came to an end in 2014. He, however, rejoined Andy Murray’s coaching staff on June 12, 2016.

Murray had won his second Wimbledon title at the end of 2016, and he had risen to the top of the world rankings.

Lendl joined Alexander Zverev’s squad in August 2018. However, due to lackluster results in 2019 and personal problems, the duo parted ways in July 2019.
